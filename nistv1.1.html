About NIST Cybersecurity Controls

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) provides comprehensive cybersecurity frameworks and guidelines that help organizations manage and reduce cybersecurity risk. These controls serve as the foundation for robust security programs across government and private sectors.

NIST's publications, particularly the Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) and Special Publication 800-53 (Security and Privacy Controls), have become authoritative references for implementing effective security controls. They provide a structured, adaptable approach that can be tailored to organizations of all sizes and across all industries.

Organizations that implement NIST-based security controls can significantly improve their security posture, demonstrate compliance with various regulations, and establish a common language for addressing cybersecurity risks.

Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

A comprehensive, flexible, and risk-based approach that helps organizations manage cybersecurity risks through a common language and methodology that's applicable across sectors and critical infrastructure.

Latest Version CSF v1.1 (2018) CSF 2.0 draft released for public comment

Framework Core Functions

The CSF Core consists of five concurrent and continuous functions that provide a strategic view of the cybersecurity risk management lifecycle. These functions work together to form a comprehensive defense strategy.

Identify

Develop organizational understanding to manage cybersecurity risk to systems, assets, data, and capabilities.

  • Asset Management: Identify and inventory all devices, software, data, and systems
  • Business Environment: Understand organization's mission, objectives, and priorities
  • Governance: Establish policies, procedures, and processes
  • Risk Assessment: Understand cybersecurity risks to operations
  • Risk Management: Establish risk tolerance and strategies

Protect

Develop and implement appropriate safeguards to ensure delivery of critical infrastructure services.

  • Identity Management: Authentication and access controls
  • Awareness & Training: Cybersecurity education for all users
  • Data Security: Protecting confidentiality, integrity, availability
  • Information Protection: Security policies and procedures
  • Protective Technology: Technical security solutions

Detect

Develop and implement appropriate activities to identify the occurrence of a cybersecurity event.

  • Anomalies & Events: Detect and analyze unusual activities
  • Security Monitoring: Continuous surveillance of systems
  • Detection Processes: Procedures to identify incidents

Respond

Develop and implement appropriate activities to take action regarding a detected cybersecurity incident.

  • Response Planning: Procedures to contain impacts
  • Communications: Coordinate with stakeholders
  • Analysis: Investigation and understanding of impacts
  • Mitigation: Actions to prevent expansion of events
  • Improvements: Incorporate lessons learned

Recover

Develop and implement appropriate activities to maintain plans for resilience and to restore services impaired by cybersecurity incidents.

  • Recovery Planning: Processes to restore systems/assets
  • Improvements: Incorporate lessons learned
  • Communications: Coordinate restoration activities

CSF Implementation Tiers: Organizational Maturity Levels

Tiers describe the degree to which an organization's cybersecurity risk management practices exhibit the characteristics defined in the Framework. They help organizations evaluate their current state and set goals for improvement.

TIER 1

Partial

Risk management practices are not formalized, and risk is managed in an ad hoc and sometimes reactive manner.

Key Characteristics
  • Limited awareness of cybersecurity risk
  • No process for collaboration or information sharing
  • Cybersecurity managed when needed, not planned
  • Reactive risk management
Example Organization: Small business with minimal IT infrastructure and no dedicated security staff.
TIER 2

Risk Informed

Risk management practices are approved by management but may not be established organization-wide policy.

Key Characteristics
  • Awareness of cybersecurity risk at management level
  • Information sharing occurs informally
  • Cybersecurity priorities may not align with risk strategy
  • Risk-informed, not risk-based decisions
Example Organization: Mid-sized company with IT department but limited dedicated security resources.
TIER 3

Repeatable

Risk management practices are formally approved and expressed as organizational policy.

Key Characteristics
  • Organization-wide approach to risk management
  • Formal information sharing policies
  • Risk-informed policies and procedures
  • Consistent methods to respond to change
Example Organization: Large enterprise with dedicated security team and established policies.
TIER 4

Adaptive

Organization adapts cybersecurity practices based on lessons learned and predictive indicators.

Key Characteristics
  • Proactive and adaptive approach to risk
  • Active information sharing with partners
  • Continuous improvement through lessons learned
  • Cybersecurity is part of organizational culture
Example Organization: Financial institution or critical infrastructure with advanced security operations.

CSF Implementation Guide: 7 Steps to Success

  1. 1

    Prioritize and Scope

    Identify business objectives and organizational priorities to make strategic decisions about cybersecurity implementation.

    Key Question: Which critical infrastructure components are most important to business mission?
  2. 2

    Orient

    Identify related systems and assets, regulatory requirements, and overall risk approach.

    Key Question: What regulatory requirements or industry frameworks must be considered?
  3. 3

    Current Profile

    Develop a Current Profile by indicating which Category and Subcategory outcomes are currently being achieved.

    Key Question: What is our organization's current cybersecurity posture?
  4. 4

    Risk Assessment

    Assess the operational environment to identify likelihood of cybersecurity events and potential impacts.

    Key Question: What are our most critical risks and vulnerabilities?
  5. 5

    Target Profile

    Create a Target Profile focused on the assessment of the Framework Categories and Subcategories describing desired cybersecurity outcomes.

    Key Question: What is our target state for cybersecurity?
  6. 6

    Determine, Analyze, and Prioritize Gaps

    Compare the Current Profile and the Target Profile to determine gaps and create a prioritized action plan.

    Key Question: What gaps exist between our current and target profiles?
  7. 7

    Implement Action Plan

    Determine which actions to take to address the gaps, and implement the plan to achieve the Target Profile outcomes.

    Key Question: How do we systematically close identified gaps?

Special Publication 800-53

A comprehensive catalog of security and privacy controls providing detailed requirements to protect the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information systems and the information processed, stored, and transmitted by those systems.

Impact Levels & Baselines

Controls are selected based on the system impact level (Low, Moderate, High) which correlates to the potential impact of a security breach on organizational operations, assets, or individuals.

Low Impact Baseline ~170 Controls

Limited adverse effect on operations, assets, or individuals

Moderate Impact Baseline ~320 Controls

Serious adverse effect on operations, assets, or individuals

High Impact Baseline ~420 Controls

Severe or catastrophic effect on operations, assets, or individuals

Tailoring Baseline Controls

Organizations should tailor the baseline controls to address:

  • Specific mission requirements
  • Operational environments
  • Unique threat scenarios
  • Specialized security needs
  • Compensating controls

Implementation Approach

SP 800-53 promotes a risk-based approach, where controls are selected, tailored, and implemented based on organizational needs and risk assessments.

Risk Management Framework (RMF) Process

  1. 1
    Categorize information system based on impact analysis
  2. 2
    Select appropriate security control baseline and apply tailoring
  3. 3
    Implement security controls and document design/implementation
  4. 4
    Assess security controls to determine effectiveness
  5. 5
    Authorize information system operation based on risk determination
  6. 6
    Monitor security controls continuously and address changes

Implementation Considerations

  • Common Controls: Security capabilities inherited by multiple systems
  • System-Specific Controls: Controls implemented for a particular system
  • Hybrid Controls: Combination of common and system-specific implementations
  • Control Parameters: Values that make controls specific to organization or system

Frequently Asked Questions

Most organizations should start with the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) as it provides a high-level, flexible approach to managing cybersecurity risk. The CSF can then be supplemented with more detailed frameworks like SP 800-53 or SP 800-171 based on your specific regulatory requirements or industry needs. The CSF is designed to be adaptable to organizations of all sizes and sectors.

NIST frameworks are compatible with and complementary to other standards like ISO 27001. While ISO 27001 provides an information security management system framework with a certification option, NIST frameworks tend to be more prescriptive with detailed security controls and implementation guidance. Many organizations implement both, using NIST for detailed security control guidance while maintaining ISO 27001 certification for business and compliance purposes. NIST provides mapping documents to help organizations relate their frameworks to other standards.

For most private sector organizations, implementation of NIST frameworks is voluntary. However, there are important exceptions. Federal contractors, especially those handling controlled unclassified information (CUI), are typically required to implement NIST SP 800-171 (via DFARS 252.204-7012 for DoD contractors). Organizations in regulated industries like healthcare or financial services may also be required to implement aspects of NIST frameworks to satisfy regulatory requirements. Even when not mandatory, NIST frameworks are widely recognized as best practices for cybersecurity.

NIST provides extensive resources to assist with framework implementation, including:

  • Reference implementations and case studies
  • Self-assessment tools and templates
  • Framework mapping guides to relate NIST controls to other standards
  • Implementation guides tailored to specific industry sectors
  • Online courses and webinars
  • The National Cybersecurity Center of Excellence (NCCoE) for collaborative solutions

All these resources are freely available on the NIST website.

NIST frameworks are typically updated every 3-5 years to respond to evolving threats, technological changes, and industry feedback. For example, the CSF was first released in 2014 (version 1.0), updated in 2018 (version 1.1), with version 2.0 currently in development. SP 800-53 has undergone five major revisions since its introduction, with the latest (Revision 5) released in 2020. NIST maintains a transparent update process that includes public comments and input from stakeholders across government, industry, and academia.

Implementation Priority Guidelines

NIST recommends implementing controls based on priority levels to address the most critical security risks first.

Priority Levels

P0: Critical Priority

Controls that address high-impact risks, vulnerabilities, or regulatory requirements. These should be implemented first.

Key families: Access Control (AC), Audit and Accountability (AU), Configuration Management (CM)

P1: High Priority

Controls that address significant security risks and should be implemented after P0 controls are in place.

Key families: Risk Assessment (RA), System and Communications Protection (SC)

P2: Moderate Priority

Controls that address moderate security risks or that enhance the overall security posture of the organization.

Key families: Awareness and Training (AT), Maintenance (MA)

P3: Low Priority

Controls that provide additional protection but address lower security risks or are more complex to implement.

Key families: Planning (PL), Program Management (PM)

Implementation Strategy

Phased Implementation Approach

  1. P0
    Phase 1: Critical Controls

    Implement all P0 controls first to establish a baseline security posture and address immediate risks. This includes fundamental access control, configuration management, and audit capabilities.

  2. P1
    Phase 2: High Priority Controls

    Once P0 controls are in place, implement P1 controls to address significant risks. This includes advanced system protection, risk assessment, and incident response mechanisms.

  3. P2
    Phase 3: Moderate Priority Controls
  4. P2
    Phase 3: Moderate Priority Controls

    Implement P2 controls to enhance security posture with awareness programs, maintenance controls, and additional safeguards that address moderate risks.

  5. P3
    Phase 4: Low Priority Controls

    Finally, implement P3 controls to complete the security program, addressing lower-risk areas and establishing long-term planning and program management controls.

Implementation Tip

Organizations should tailor control implementation based on their unique risk profile and business needs. The prioritization framework provides guidance, but a risk assessment should inform the final implementation strategy.

Implementation Tip

Coverage Analysis Summary

NIST provides comprehensive coverage of the OWASP Top 10 risks, with detailed controls that address all major web application security concerns. However, OWASP offers more specific guidance for developers on implementation details and coding practices.

OWASP focuses primarily on application security controls and doesn't address many organizational and management controls found in NIST frameworks. Using both together provides the best coverage of both technical and governance aspects of security.

Implementation Methodology

A structured approach to implementing NIST security controls in your organization, following industry best practices and NIST guidance.

1

Assessment & Scoping

Begin with a comprehensive assessment of your current security posture and define the scope of your NIST implementation.

Key Activities

  • Conduct security gap assessment
  • Evaluate current control effectiveness
  • Identify applicable regulations and requirements
  • Define implementation boundary and scope
  • Categorize systems (FIPS 199) by impact level

Deliverables

  • Gap analysis report
  • System categorization document
  • Implementation scope statement
  • Current security posture baseline
  • Stakeholder roles and responsibilities
2

Control Selection & Tailoring

Select the appropriate security controls based on your risk assessment and system categorization, then tailor them to your organization's unique needs.

Key Activities

  • Select baseline controls (Low/Moderate/High)
  • Apply scoping considerations
  • Implement control tailoring guidance
  • Document control parameter selections
  • Develop organization-defined values

Deliverables

  • Tailored control baseline
  • Control tailoring rationale
  • Security control implementation statements
  • Compensating control documentation
  • Control inheritance documentation

Implementation Tip

When selecting controls, consider inheriting common controls from the broader organization rather than implementing from scratch for each system. Document which controls are common (organization-wide), system-specific, or hybrid.

3

Implementation Planning

Develop a comprehensive implementation plan that prioritizes controls based on risk, available resources, and organizational constraints.

Execute the implementation plan by deploying technical solutions, developing and enforcing policies and procedures, and training staff.

Technical Controls

  • Configure systems securely

  • Establish technical architecture

  • Develop security procedures

  • Align with broader risk management
  • Develop meaningful security metrics

  • Automate control implementation where possible
  • Implement security-as-code practices
  • Develop standardized security configurations
  • Create control testing frameworks

Organizational Practices

  • Implement incentives for security practices
  • Create security communities of practice

Common Implementation Challenges

  • Resource Constraints

    Limited budget, personnel, or expertise to fully implement controls

    Solution: Prioritize based on risk and implement in phases

  • Complex Technical Environments

    Legacy systems, heterogeneous environments, and technical debt

    Solution: Use compensating controls and modernization roadmaps

  • Resistance to Change

    Organizational inertia and resistance to new security measures

    Solution: Focus on awareness and gradual implementation

  • Documentation Burden

    Extensive documentation requirements that strain resources

    Solution: Use documentation tools and templates, automate where possible

  • Balancing Security and Operations

    Security controls that impede business functions and productivity

    Solution: Design with usability in mind and involve business stakeholders

  • Keeping Pace with Changes

    Rapidly evolving technology and threat landscapes

    Solution: Implement agile security practices and continuous monitoring
